
Step 7 consolidates the entire SACRED process into a **Safe Operating Concept (SOC)**.

Step 7.1 defines the SOC as a set of **explicit operating conditions**.

---

### Purpose

Define when autonomous operation is permitted.

Each condition:

- is derived from hazards, metrics and technological limits,
- is expressed as a clear requirement,
- and defines part of the system’s admissible operating envelope.

---

### Approach

All constraints identified in earlier steps are translated into requirement statements.

These include:

- **Metrics (Step 4)** → quantitative limits  
- **Failure responses (Step 5)** → required behaviour under degradation  
- **Technology bounds (Step 6)** → capability constraints  

Requirements are expressed in a consistent structure:

> *<conditions> the system shall <behaviour>*

This allows operational boundaries to be defined as:

- always-on constraints  
- state-based conditions  
- event-triggered responses  
- degraded or failure conditions  

---

### Example: Newcastle Corridor

The Safe Operating Concept for Newcastle is defined as a set of boundary conditions.

Each condition represents a constraint that must hold for autonomy to be valid.

---

### SoC-1: Domain and Authority Boundary

Autonomy is limited to the scoped operational domain and remains subject to railway authority structures.

**Condition**

- Operation is restricted to the defined route and direction  
- Movement authority is provided by signalling infrastructure  

**Requirement**

> The system shall operate only within the defined corridor and shall treat signalling systems as authoritative for movement permission.
