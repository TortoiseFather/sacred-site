
Deliverable D5 is produced in Step 2 and classifies each causal component identified in the fault trees (D4) according to where responsibility lies: the **ego-vehicle**, the **ecosystem**, or a **shared interface between the two**.

This classification is central to the SACRED methodology, as it explicitly separates what the autonomous system can control from what it must respond to. It operationalises the *ego-within-ecosystem* framing by assigning each causal element to its source of influence.

---

## Classification Categories

Each node in the fault tree (D4) is assigned to one of the following:

### Ego-Vehicle

A causal component is assigned to the **ego-vehicle** when it falls within the capabilities of the onboard system, given:
- Available sensing  
- Braking performance  
- Protection and control systems  

These are factors the vehicle can directly perceive, decide upon, and act to correct within its operational envelope.

---

### Ecosystem

A component is assigned to the **ecosystem** when the condition arises from:
- Fixed infrastructure  
- Environmental exposure  
- External operational processes  

These are factors that cannot be directly sensed, corrected, or compensated for by the vehicle within the available **sighting distance** or **braking envelope** defined by the route.

In traditional railway operation, these conditions are typically managed externally (e.g. by signalling systems or operators), with the train reacting to communicated authority or constraints.

---

### Shared (Contextual)

Some components involve **sequential or conditional dependence** between the ecosystem and the ego-vehicle.

These are classified as **shared**, where:
- An ecosystem-level condition occurs  
- The ego-vehicle has the ability to respond or recover  

The assignment in these cases is **context-dependent**, and is tied to the operational state at which the transition between ecosystem condition and ego response occurs.

---

## Purpose

D5 is important because it:
- Clarifies **responsibility boundaries** within the system  
- Identifies where autonomy must act versus where it must defer  
- Supports requirement allocation in Step 3  
- Helps structure later metrics and assurance arguments  

By explicitly separating ego and ecosystem contributions, D5 ensures that autonomy is not over-scoped beyond what can realistically be controlled from the cab.

---

## Example

For a hazard such as **SPAD (H-01)**, the Eco–Ego assignment might look like:

- **Reduced adhesion** → Ecosystem  
- **Brake system performance** → Ego  
- **Signal visibility (line of sight)** → Ecosystem  
- **Signal interpretation** → Ego  
- **Delayed reaction to signal** → Ego  
- **Late braking due to external disruption** → Shared  

This classification makes clear which parts of the hazard pathway are controllable by the autonomous system and which must be treated as external constraints.
