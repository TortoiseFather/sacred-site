
Deliverable D4 is produced in Step 2 and provides high-level causal explorations for selected hazards identified in the Hazard Register (D3). Rather than attempting to model every possible failure pathway, D4 focuses on identifying the **direct upstream conditions** that can lead to a hazard occurring.

Each fault tree is centred on a specific hazard and works backwards from the point at which that hazard manifests. The aim is not exhaustive enumeration, but to expose **recurring causal themes and dependency patterns** that can later inform:
- Requirement elicitation in Step 3  
- Component and state identification in later parts of Step 2  

Importantly, D4 is intentionally scoped to remain simple and interpretable. It avoids complex representations such as full “Swiss cheese” models or deeply layered failure chains.

---

## Key Principles

- Focus on **first-order causes** that arise during the relevant operational phase  
- Anchor the tree to a **specific hazard boundary** defined in D3  
- Keep the structure **acyclic and directed**, terminating at the hazard  
- Group causes into **qualitatively distinct themes** (e.g. perception, braking, authority, environment)  
- Do **not** include:
  - Downstream consequences  
  - Mitigation strategies  
  - Responsibility allocation  

D4 is a structured way of understanding *how a hazard can arise*, not what happens after or how it is handled.

---

## Process

To construct a fault tree for a given hazard:

1. **Select the hazard** from the Hazard Register (D3)  
2. **Fix the operational milestone** where the hazard occurs (e.g. signal approach, braking phase)  
3. **Define the hazard boundary** (the point at which the hazard is realised)  
4. **Work backwards** to identify credible first-order upstream conditions  
5. **Group conditions into causal themes**  
6. **Represent the structure** as a directed, acyclic tree terminating at the hazard  

---

## Example

For **H-01: Signal Passed at Danger (SPAD)** during a signal approach phase, a simplified fault tree might include:

- **Hazard: SPAD (failure to stop before red signal)**  
  - Inadequate braking performance  
    - Reduced adhesion  
    - Brake system degradation  
  - Late or failed perception of signal  
    - Obstructed line of sight  
    - Sensor misclassification  
  - Incorrect authority interpretation  
    - Signal state misread  
    - Data interface inconsistency  

Each branch represents a **first-order causal pathway** that could lead to the hazard within the defined operational context. These pathways are not exhaustive, but they highlight the dominant causal structure that will later be translated into requirements and measurable constraints.
