
In Step 6.1, we identify technologies that *might* support the system.

Step 6.2 is where we test the theory. Technologies are typically described under controlled conditions. In real railway operation, performance depends on environment, context, and interaction with the wider system. If these limits are not made explicit, the safety argument rests on assumptions rather than evidence.

We can address this by applying **TRAP** to each technology.

---

### TRAP for Technology

For each system (e.g. perception, adhesion monitoring, communication), we describe the conditions around it:

- performance depends on lighting, weather, and occlusion  
- degradation may occur gradually or silently  
- failure may appear as misclassification, not absence  
- operation may depend on external systems (e.g. signalling, communication)

Turning each capability into a bounded assumption. Determining how each technology can be used, in the format:

- **Trigger** — when the technology becomes safety-critical or begins to degrade  
- **Response** — what the system does when that condition is encountered  
- **Assurance** — what must still be true for the system to remain safe  
- **Process** — how this is recorded, communicated, or escalated  

---

### Outcome

The result of Step 6.2 is a set of **conditions under which each technology can be relied upon**.

These conditions directly shape the Safe Operating Concept:
- where autonomy is permitted  
- when behaviour must degrade  
- when responsibility shifts to the wider railway system
