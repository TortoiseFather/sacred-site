## 6.5 Boundary Definition

Steps 6.1–6.4 identify what technologies can do, where they work, and where they fail.  
Step 6.5 turns that into explicit operating boundaries.

---

### Purpose

Define the conditions under which autonomous operation is permitted.

These boundaries ensure that:

- safety requirements are only enforced where supporting capabilities exist,
- technological limits are made explicit,
- and responsibility is correctly assigned between the onboard system and the railway ecosystem.

---

### Activities

1. Combine results from:
   - Technology Scoping (what exists)
   - SotA TRAP (how it fails)
   - Operational State Validation (where it applies)
   - Simulation & Experimentation (what actually happens)

2. Derive constraints on:
   - sensing range  
   - braking capability  
   - localisation integrity  
   - communication reliability  
   - environmental conditions  

3. Identify where:
   - autonomy is supported,
   - degraded operation is required,
   - or ecosystem support is mandatory  

4. Define these constraints as operational rules for the Safe Operating Concept.

---

### Example: Newcastle Corridor

Applying Boundary Definition refines the Safe Operating Concept into concrete constraints.

#### 1. Obstacle Detection vs Stopping Distance

Requirement:
- Hazard detection must occur within the stopping envelope

Finding:
- No strong evidence that onboard perception alone can reliably detect hazards at required distances

Boundary:
- Operation must either:
  - occur within reduced speeds, or  
  - rely on ecosystem sensing (e.g. trackside monitoring)

---

#### 2. Authority Resolution

Requirement:
- Movement authority must be resolved before a braking deadline

Finding:
- Structured signalling is reliable  
- Open-ended communication is not

Boundary:
- Authority must be resolved through structured signalling systems  
- No reliance on free-form human-style dialogue

---

#### 3. Adhesion and Braking Capability

Requirement:
- Braking performance must remain within a known safe bound

Finding:
- Runtime adhesion estimation is feasible with reasonable accuracy

Boundary:
- Operation assumes a conservative lower bound on adhesion  
- If adhesion cannot be bounded → transition to degraded behaviour  

---

#### 4. Hazard Response

Finding:
- Some systems (e.g. braking, horn) respond reliably once hazards are detected  
- Detection itself is not always guaranteed at sufficient range  

Boundary:
- Some safety mechanisms are treated as **mitigation**, not prevention  
- Full safety depends on coordination with the wider ecosystem  

## D18: State-of-the-Art Capability Assessment

A structured summary of what is technologically supported.

For each requirement:

- **Supported**  
  Capability is consistent with demonstrated technology  
  (e.g. adhesion monitoring)

- **Partially Supported**  
  Capability exists but with limitations or uncertainty  
  (e.g. perception range)

- **Ecosystem Dependent**  
  Capability cannot be satisfied onboard alone  
  (e.g. long-range hazard detection, authority resolution context)


### Key Result

Autonomy is defined by **what can be guaranteed**, not what is theoretically possible.

Boundary Definition ensures that every condition in the Safe Operating Concept is backed by:

- a demonstrated capability,  
- a bounded assumption, or  
- an explicit dependency on the railway ecosystem.
