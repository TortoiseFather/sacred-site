
Deliverable D3 is produced in Step 2 and captures the set of hazards identified through the scoping of both the ego-vehicle and the wider ecosystem. It represents the first structured articulation of what can go wrong within the defined operational context.

Hazards are recorded in a standardised form, typically labelled as **H-xx**, and are derived from the interaction between:
- The operational behaviour of the ego-vehicle  
- The constraints and features of the ecosystem  
- The structure imposed by the Operational Design Model (ODM) and Scenario Definition (D1)  

A key characteristic of D3 is that hazards are not abstract. They are **anchored to operational milestones** defined within the ODM and scenario progression. This ensures that hazards are tied to specific points in operation, such as approaching a signal, entering a junction, or initiating braking, rather than being treated as decontextualised risks.

D3 therefore moves the process from *what the system does* (D1) to *what can go wrong while it does it*, grounded in the actual structure of the railway environment.

---

## Structure

A hazard entry in D3 typically includes:
- **Hazard ID (H-xx)**  
- **Description of the hazard**  
- **Associated operational milestone or context**  
- **Relevant ego and/or ecosystem components**  

This provides a consistent way to track and reference hazards throughout the remainder of the SACRED process.

---

## Example

A simplified hazard register might include entries such as:

- **H-01: Signal Passed at Danger (SPAD)**  
  Occurs when the train fails to stop before a red signal during an approach phase.

- **H-08: Adhesion Loss**  
  Reduced wheel–rail adhesion affecting braking performance during deceleration.

- **H-11: Authority Ambiguity**  
  Uncertainty in movement authority due to conflicting or unclear signalling information.

Each of these hazards is tied to a specific operational context (e.g. signal approach, braking phase), ensuring that they can later be analysed, decomposed, and translated into requirements and measurable constraints.
