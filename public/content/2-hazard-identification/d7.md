
Deliverable D7 represents the **Verified Operational Design Model (ODM)** and is produced iteratively throughout the SACRED process. It captures the system’s current, validated understanding of the environment in which it operates.

The ODM models the world as it is *understood by the system*. In the railway domain, this can be defined on a **per-route basis**, reflecting both:

- **Static infrastructure**  
  (e.g. signals, junctions, gradients, overpasses, stations)

- **Dynamic factors**  
  (e.g. other trains, pedestrians, vehicles on overpasses, environmental conditions)

Because railway environments are relatively fixed and structured compared to road systems, the ODM can be scoped with a high degree of specificity. However, it must still evolve as understanding improves.

---

## Iterative Nature

The Verified ODM is not a fixed document — it is **iterative** and evolves alongside the methodology.

- At the end of **Step 2 (Hazard Identification)**, the ODM reflects:
  - The initial scenario definition (D1)  
  - The hazard structures (D3–D6)  
  - The Eco–Ego assignments (D5)  

  At this stage, it represents a **baseline, hazard-informed understanding of the route**.

- By the end of **Step 4 (Metrics that Matter)**, the Verified ODM is updated to include:
  - System requirements  
  - Measurable constraints and bounds  
  - The minimum conditions required to ensure safe operation  

  At this point, the ODM becomes a **validated representation of the environment with embedded assurance constraints**.

---

## Purpose

D7 is important because it:

- Provides a **consistent reference model** of the environment  
- Ensures that all hazards, requirements, and metrics are grounded in a shared representation  
- Captures both **what exists** in the environment and **what must hold true** for safe operation  
- Evolves alongside the system as assumptions are refined and verified  

Rather than being a static description, the Verified ODM becomes a **living model of the route**, progressively refined as more evidence, constraints, and requirements are introduced.

---

## Example

For a route such as **Swalwell Junction → Newcastle**, the Verified ODM might include:

- Signal locations, aspects, and visibility constraints  
- Track geometry and gradients affecting braking performance  
- Station positions and stopping requirements  
- Known environmental factors (e.g. areas prone to low adhesion)  
- Interactions with other traffic and signalling systems  

At later stages, this same ODM would also encode:

- Minimum sighting distances required for safe signal detection  
- Braking performance bounds under varying conditions  
- Constraints derived from hazard analysis and system requirements
